# 08 — Kids / Baby

Mostly allowed; targeting and imagery rules are the most common audit findings.

## Hard rules

❌ Banned:
- **Targeting users under 18** with ads (regardless of product)
- Sexualized / inappropriate imagery of minors (this should be obvious)
- Treating children as the buyer in ad copy (kids aren't the buyer)
- Health / safety claims without certification (FDA-cleared, JPMA-certified, ASTM)
- Toys with banned materials / non-compliant safety standards (varies by region)

✅ Allowed:
- Targeting parents (25–55 generally, with kids in household)
- Baby products of all kinds (formula has its own rules)
- Educational toys / STEM / books
- Children's clothing (with appropriate imagery)
- Maternity / pregnancy products

## Targeting reality

Meta's Detailed Targeting category "parents (children at home)" is allowed. "Parents (specific child age)" is allowed but narrowed in 2025. Lookalikes from purchasers are allowed.

## Formula-specific (own restrictions)

- Infant formula (0–6 months) is restricted in many regions (WHO Code countries). Audit insight: a brand running formula ads in WHO Code countries (most of Europe, parts of Asia) without a proper exemption is at risk.
- Toddler formula and follow-on milk: less restricted but still scrutinized.
- Marketing claims on cognitive / immune benefits often disapproved.

## Banned creative patterns

- Imagery suggesting milk-bottle is preferable to breastfeeding (in WHO Code regions)
- Health claims on formula ("makes baby smarter")
- Imagery of distressed children
- "Limited time" / scarcity copy on baby essentials
- Anything implying bad parenting if the product isn't bought

## Public-scraping red flags

| Red flag | Severity |
|---|---|
| Formula brand running in WHO Code countries | Critical |
| Health claims on formula / baby food | Critical |
| Personal-attribute prompts to parents ("Are you a tired mom?") | High |
| Imagery of distressed / crying children | High |
| Targeting that implies under-18 buyer | Critical |
| Missing safety certifications on LP | High |
| Cognitive / behavioral claims without studies | High |

## Landing-page checks

| Element | Required for |
|---|---|
| Safety certifications (CPSC, JPMA, ASTM, EN-71) | Toys |
| Allergen disclosures | Food / formula |
| Age-grading | All toys / equipment |
| Warning labels (small parts, choking hazard) | All applicable products |
| Privacy policy with COPPA mention if data collected | Apps / digital products for kids |

## Creative norms that work

- Real-life parent / lifestyle moments
- Educational benefit framing ("counts as screen-free time")
- Founder-mom story
- Heritage / quality (long-time toy brands)
- Safety + certifications first
- "Designed by [pediatrician/educator]" with named credentials
- Subscription / convenience framing

## What doesn't work / gets killed

- Health claims without RCT-level studies
- "Top scientific" framing without citations
- Influencer copy that names benefits the brand can't prove
- Sad-baby imagery
- Aggressive scarcity / FOMO

## Sample-report wording template

> **Compliance — Kids / Baby**
> Two findings:
> - Your formula ads run in `<EU country>`; under the WHO International Code (which most EU member states have implemented), infant-formula advertising is restricted. Even when Meta approves the ads, regional regulators may issue takedowns.
> - Ad `<id>` claims `<verbatim>` — cognitive benefit claims on baby food / formula need either FDA-recognized studies or a softer framing ("provides DHA, an essential nutrient for development").
